    Analytic Geometry

    Analytic geometry, also known as coordinate geometry or Cartesian geometry, is a branch of mathematics that combines Algebra and Geometry to study geometric objects and their properties using a coordinate system.

    Core Concepts

    • Coordinate System
      Geometric shapes and figures are represented numerically using points on a plane or in space.
    • Line
    • Slope
    • Conic Section
    • Representation of points, lines, and shapes in coordinate systems.
    • Distance, slope, and intersections.
    • Geometric relationships and their algebraic equivalents.

    Key Features

    • Coordinate System:
      • In two dimensions, the Cartesian coordinate system is defined by two perpendicular axes, $x$ (horizontal) and $y$ (vertical).
      • In three dimensions, the system includes an additional $z$-axis for depth.
    • Algebraic Representation:
      Points, lines, curves, and surfaces are described using algebraic equations, allowing geometric problems to be solved algebraically.

    Scope of Analytic Geometry

    It encompasses:

    • Points and their coordinates.
    • Lines and their equations.
    • Planes in three dimensions.
    • Curves like circles, parabolas, ellipses, and hyperbolas (conic sections).
    • Distance, slope, and midpoint formulas.
    • Intersections, tangents, and normals.
    • Vector representations in space.

    Applications

    Analytic geometry is foundational in:

    • Engineering and physics for modeling physical systems.
    • Computer graphics for rendering shapes and spaces.
    • Robotics and navigation for understanding positions and movements.
    • Practical problems in 2D and 3D geometry, such as intersections, tangents, and trajectories.
