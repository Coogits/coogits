    Electric Flux

    Electric flux ($\Phi_E$) quantifies the total electric field passing through a given surface. It measures how much of the electric field penetrates or "flows" through a surface and is crucial in understanding Gauss's Law.

    Symbols

    • $\Phi_E\quad$ Electric Flux, representing the total electric field passing through a surface (unit:
    • $\vec E\quad$ Electric Field, describing the field's strength and direction
    • $\vec A\quad$ Area Vector, whose magnitude is the surface area and direction is normal to the surface (unit: $m^2$).
    • $Q_{enc}\quad$ Enclosed Charge, the total Electric Charge inside a closed surface (unit: Coulomb, C).
    • $\epsilon_0\quad$ vacuum permittivity, a fundamental constant ($\epsilon_0\simeq8,854\cdot10^{-12}\;\frac{C^2}{N\cdot m^2}$)

    Formulas

    • Electric Flux in a Uniform Field:
      For a uniform electric field $\vec E$ passing through a flat surface of area A , the flux is given by the Dot Product (Vector): $$\Phi_E=\vec{E}\cdot\vec{A}=\lvert\vec{E}\rvert\cdot\lvert\vec{A}\rvert\cos\theta$$
    • Electric Flux in a Non-Uniform Field:
      When the electric field varies over the surface, the total flux is obtained by integrating over the entire surface S: $$\Phi_E=\int_s\vec E\;d\vec A$$
    • Gauss's Law:
      Gauss's Law relates the total electric flux through a closed surface to the net charge enclosed within it: $$\int_s\vec E\;d\vec A=\cfrac{Q_{enc}}{\epsilon_0}$$

    Unit

    • SI Unit: Is Newton-quare meters per Coulombs: $$\Phi_E=\left(\cfrac{N}{C}\right)\cdot (m^2)=\cfrac{N\cdot m^2}{C}$$
