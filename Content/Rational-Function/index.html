    Rational Function

    A rational function is a function that is expressed as the ratio of two polynomials.

    Symbols

    • $P(x)\quad$ Polynomial which can be any polynomial (constant or non-constant).
    • $Q(x)\quad$ Polynomial that must contain at least one variable (i.e., not a constant zero) and must satisfy $Q\neq0$

    Formulas

    • Definition:
      It is defined as: $$f(x)=\cfrac{P(x)}{Q(x)}$$

    Properties

    • The domain includes all real numbers except where $Q(x)=0$ (which creates vertical asymptotes or holes).

    Special Cases

    • Polynomial Functions as Rational Functions
      A polynomial is a special case of a rational function where the denominator is $Q(x)=1$: $$f(x)=\cfrac{P(x)}{1}$$
    • Polynomial Functions as Reciprocal Function:
      A rational function where the numerator is 1 and the denominator is a linear monomial: $$f(x)=\cfrac{1}{x}$$

    Proper Rational Function

    A rational function where the degree of the numerator $P(x)$ is less than the degree of the denominator $Q(x)$. $$\text{deg}P(x)<\text{deg}Q(x)$$

    Improper Rational Function

    A rational function where the degree of the numerator $P(x)$ is greater than or equal to the degree of the denominator $Q(x)$. $$\text{deg}P(x)>\text{deg}Q(x)$$

    Asymptotic behavior

    • $\text{deg}P(x)<\text{deg}Q(x)$ (Proper Rational Function)
      If the degree of $P(x)$ is less than the degree of $Q(x)$, the function has a horizontal asymptote at $y=0$.
    • $\text{deg}P(x)=\text{deg}Q(x)$
      If the degrees of $P(x)$ and $Q(x)$ are equal, the horizontal asymptote is at: $$y=\cfrac{a_P}{a_Q}$$ where
      • $a_P\quad$ Leading Coefficient of $P(x)$
      • $a_Q\quad$ Leading Coefficient of $Q(x)$
    • $\text{deg}P(x)>\text{deg}Q(x)$ (improper Rational Function)
      If the degree of $P(x)$ is greater than $Q(x)$, the function does not have a horizontal asymptote. Instead:
      • $\text{deg}P(x)=\text{deg}Q(x)+1$
        the function has an oblique (slant) asymptote.
      • $\text{deg}P(x)>\text{deg}Q(x)+1$
        the function behaves like the polynomial resulting from division at large x, meaning it follows the shape of the quotient polynomial as $x\to+\infty$
